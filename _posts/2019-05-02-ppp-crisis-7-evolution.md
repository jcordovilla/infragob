### RECAPITULATION AND REFLECTIONS ON A BETTER GOVERNANCE OF INFRASTRUCTURE PPPs.

Private participation in infrastructure provision has been a cyclical process since the first industrial revolution, and in many cases the private sector has actually preceded the government in the development and management of infrastructure. After an alternating series of waves of nationalisation and privatisation, the beginning of the 21st century saw the rise of the public-private partnership (PPP) model, as a consequence of the sovereign credit crisis in developing countries and the subsequent reforms and instruments promoted by the multilateral institutions, most notably development banks and the OECD.

Legislation, institutional capacity, knowledge and investment in PPPs thrived during the period between 2004 and 2012, when they became the golden promise for infrastructure delivery. However, and against the logic of all the potential benefits that PPPs should bring, there was a drastic reversal in trend -both in number of new projects and in total investment- of this type of public procurement since 2012. Global GDP has increased by approximately 30% since 2012, and while government investment in infrastructure has remained at around 2% of GDP per annum, the proportion that goes to PPP investment has plummeted from 8% to less than 1%. The reasons for this debacle are the dynamics that take hold of the seemingly robust and sophisticated legal and financial mechanisms behind PPPs when they meet the complex realities of globalisation and human behaviour.

Public-private partnerships are chosen for many reasons, but three pillars stand out, namely: 1) access to more financing for infrastructure; 2) efficiency and reliability gains brought by the private sector; and 3) long-term benefits such as regional wealth creation, private sector competitiveness, government capacity development and knowledge exchange.

It turns out, however, that the results are often the opposite. For all efforts and attention dedicated to the sophistication of the contractual arrangements and financial flows, the conventional PPP model has neglected issues that fundamentally erode the partnership in the long term. Furthermore, the stark contradiction between promises and results has undermined the confidence in the model.

I have reviewed this contradiction and the dynamics that kill the financing, efficiency and long-term value arguments of PPPs in parts Three, Four and Five of this monographic series, respectively. The bottom line: conventional PPPs have failed to deliver because they have fundamental flaws in the way that they are designed, implemented and managed.

> "The prevailing PPP model is ill-suited to the infrastructure need of today's societies, so it must evolve towards a model that is adaptive, functional, and transparent."

Infrastructure development urgently needs partnerships where the capacities, behaviours and resources of the people in charge of infrastructure delivery get as much attention as the legal and financial arrangements.

The public sector must ensure they have the people and institutions capable of governing infrastructure investments and sustaining public value in the long term, before these investments are sought from the private markets.

The relationship with the private sector and other actors (interest groups, NGOs, public managers, political leaders) should rely on strategic plans for transparency and use of information, agreed before drafting complex agreements. Such plans should state the capacities, interests and responsibilities of the parties involved, aligning them strategically towards the common goals in a way that everyone understands and adopts the added value of managed transparency, thus reducing suspicion and fear of "revealing" valuable information.

Given the transformative power of information technologies, the best governance models will be those that make the flow of information more effective and responsible. By recognizing information as an asset as valuable as the infrastructure itself, we will open a field of possibilities, the use of the value of data to finance infrastructure directly, but there are important risks to manage, notably personal data protection and data safety. Institutions such as the Open Data Institute have come a long way in creating open standards that help each stakeholder manage their data strategically, choosing whether and which data should be closed, open or shared, with a variety of effective exchange mechanisms (commercial and non-commercial) that keep information safe and well governed.

The main obstacle to modern partnerships between the public and the private sector in infrastructure is that data are not truly shared, and -as noted by my friends at IcebreakerOne- the non-technological people in the infrastructure sector (that is, most of us) have not worked out yet how to truly share data. In the age of abundance and complexity, there will be no successful infrastructure PPPs unless the parties have established a data partnership first. This is only possible if collaboration standards are in place.

It is extremely difficult to guarantee a total absence of conflicts of interest. Even so, the very exercise of identifying potential conflicts of interest -disclosure- and anticipating scenarios is a self-reinforcing tool for collaboration that avoids surprises and can limit unwanted behaviours when the circumstances change in the future. Besides, we should get used to demanding the same standards of transparency from public bodies as from private firms, within the limits that ensure security and competitiveness.

The use of clear, concise language to describe common objectives, risks and relationships between the stakeholders is essential to maintain trust in collaboration. In the same way as for data, we must address the issue of language and communication strategically, right from the start, before the negotiation of terms and conditions. For language, in the long term, becomes a driving force in managing expectations -including those of the user-, resolving conflicts and sustaining the legitimacy of the partnership.

> "The promotion of a clear, common language that focuses on shared objectives and interests- and not exclusively on financial, legal and technical complexities- could become the cornerstone of a cultural change in public-private collaboration."

Technically, we have plenty of tools and techniques to elucidate what infrastructure investments are required and how the service can best be provided and evaluated, right from the beginning. The obstacle is one of culture and skills. The construction sector has a long and fascinating way ahead to boost innovation and productivity, but the inertia of "business as usual" is powerful and keeps drawing us towards the choice of monolithic solutions, which must then be shored up by sophisticated contractual instruments. And these instruments are, in essence, fragile: they are not equipped -as was discussed in previous posts- to react to the dynamics of value creation or erosion, nor do they have the capacity to adapt to substantial change.

By monolithic solution I mean the bundling of elements that have great disparities in life cycle needs, functional features, accounting rules or legal considerations into a single contract, solely because of the economies of scale. We need to start questioning that this be the optimal solution, and in some cases not even a viable option.

There is a long way of trial and error towards the conception of highly adaptive PPP models. For a start, we could unbundle infrastructure projects into elements and processes, not based on financial or efficiency criteria only, but also on the role they play in achieving long-term outcomes. This exercise requires substantial analytical resources, but an even bigger challenge is the management of the increasing number of interfaces that result from disaggregation. Hence the need for new standards, protocols and processes to facilitate smooth interactions while preserving the integrity of the system.

Technologies like blockchain -a type of distributed ledger that can cryptographically "seal" a contract or transaction- are revealing very interesting options to automate functionality, transparency and security in contractual relations between parties with very dissimilar interests. Such technologies reinforce interfaces and eliminate intermediaries and their associated costs, which so far have inhibited the adoption of small-scale PPPs. Yet this paradigm shift begins with a change of mentality in the sector.

A possible natural evolution of the current PPP model is towards one with greater presence of the private partner in components that have both shorter useful lifespans and a direct impact on efficiency gains, essentially alike the short and medium-term nature of the private company. The public partner, on the other hand, would assume components with longer useful lives and lesser room for efficiency gains, or else wherever efficiency is not a first-order matter, in consistency with the intergenerational responsibility. The design phase of the PPP structure would also factor in externalities (positive and negative) and intangible assets such as reputation or social legitimacy.

But above all is the need for transparency and communication, especially with the users and citizens, who -technically skilled or not- should be clearly informed about what infrastructure is needed, how it is paid and how it is operated.

> "You have to get used to accepting that the future is impossible to predict, even more so in an increasingly uncertain and complex environment. Basing enormous contractual structures on thirty-year forecasts is a guarantee of failure."

Ventures and partnerships are much more likely to endure if they rely on strategic analysis and scenario planning, practices that are becoming commonplace in private sector business continuity plans. These techniques consider a wide range of scenarios, studying how the interests of the parties are affected in each case, as well as the possible choices. From here one can derive compensation mechanisms, incentives or adaptation measures that reinforce the continuity of the company and the achievement of the long-term objectives.

The principles I have set forth will surely encounter very different institutional capacities and skills, depending on the administrative scale -state, regional, local. A possible governance solution would be the establishment of centers of excellence at central administration level to establish strategic PPP policies and provide support for sub-national bodies.

**To innovate and adapt, one must be willing to think and act differently**. Humility and the ability to change perspective allow us to understand how complex problems are solved in other fields. We find convenient examples in the software and communication technologies sector, or even in art. However, the mere adoption of instruments or technologies that tech entrepreneurs and professionals use to collaborate will not suffice: The real engine of the tech industry is the talent, attitude and values of its people.

The transformation towards a new model of public-private collaboration in infrastructure needs innovative instruments, but moreover it needs talent, attitudes and values that foster valuable and lasting collaboration ventures.
